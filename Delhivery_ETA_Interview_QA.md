## 1. "Walk me through this project" — framing

**Q1. In two minutes, what problem were you solving and why does it matter?**
A: Delhivery's routing engine (OSRM) systematically underestimates delivery time — median actual time is 1.86x the OSRM estimate, and 94.7% of trips breach the 1.2x SLA threshold. That's not noise, it's systemic. I modeled the logistics network as a directed graph (1,588 facilities, ~2,500 corridors) to find *which hubs* cause disproportionate downstream delay, engineered 69 features capturing hub dwell time and corridor reliability that OSRM is blind to, and built an ensemble ETA model that improved accuracy (% of trips predicted within 15% of actual time) from 54.1% to 74.2%, while quantifying ₹149L in revenue-at-risk concentrated in 3 hubs.

**Q2. Why is this a graph problem and not just regression?**
A: Distance and OSRM time tell you about a single edge; they can't tell you that a hub sits on 36% of all shortest paths in the network (Gurgaon's betweenness) and that congestion there ripples into hundreds of corridors simultaneously. Graph position captures *structural* risk that's invisible at the row level. I proved this wasn't just a nice idea with an ablation: adding graph centrality features alone (before any hub/corridor history) cut MAE by 5.2 minutes and lifted accuracy from 53.3% to 60.4% — graph position alone accounted for ~70% of my total model improvement.

**Q3. Trip vs. segment — why model at the segment level?**
A: A trip (14,817 unique `trip_uuid`s) is one shipment's full origin-to-destination journey. A segment is one hop of that journey through an intermediate hub — median 17 hops per trip, which is why 14,817 trips expand to 141,866 rows. I modeled at the segment level because dwell time and OSRM blind-spots are hop-level phenomena (each hub adds its own dwell), then aggregated back up — `compute_hop_features` groups by `trip_uuid` to get `num_hops`, `cumulative_osrm`, etc., and corridor stats aggregate by hub-pair for the graph edges.

**Q4. 1,588 nodes but only 1,261 in the largest WCC — what happened to the rest?**
A: About 327 facilities are either isolated or sit in small disconnected components — likely low-volume or edge-of-network destinations that only ever appear once or twice and never connect back into the main hub-and-spoke structure. Betweenness/PageRank/Node2Vec are only computed on `G_main` (the largest WCC) because centrality metrics are meaningless or undefined on disconnected components. For cold-start facilities not in `node_df` at all, every downstream `.map()` call is followed by `.fillna(0)` (see `src_bottleneck`, `src_betweenness`, etc.) so they don't break the pipeline, but it does mean those rare hubs get a "neutral"/zero bottleneck signal rather than a real one — worth flagging as a limitation if asked.

**Q5. Walk through the pipeline end-to-end.**
A: (1) Load raw CSV, compute headline stats. (2) Clean — drop ~2.07% anomalous rows (bad `factor`/`segment_factor`/negative times). (3) Tier-1 features: hub dwell (T1-A), corridor delay history (T1-B), hop complexity (T1-C), TOD-stratified corridor delay (T1-D) — all computed on train only, merged onto train+test. (4) Tier-2 features: dwell-weighted hops (T2-B), extreme-dwell flags + log transforms (T2-C), corridor risk score (T2-D), hub×TOD interaction (T2-E), explicit interaction terms (T2-F). (5) Build the directed graph with dwell-adjusted edge weights. (6) Compute betweenness/degree/PageRank/clustering/Node2Vec, derive the bottleneck score, save bottleneck hubs and chronic corridors. (7) Baseline RF models (raw vs log target). (8) Two-stage extreme/normal model. (9) Stacking ensemble (RF+XGB+LGB→Ridge). (10) Full v2→v3→v4 ablation benchmark. (11) FTL vs Carting classifier + routing rules. (12) Strategy memo generation. If asked to add a new feature, it'd slot into step 3 or 4 depending on whether it needs only raw columns (T1) or depends on other engineered features (T2).

**Q6. v1→v4 progression — what did v4 specifically buy you?**
A: v2 = OSRM/base features + graph centrality only. v3 added T1-A/B/C (hub dwell, corridor history, hop complexity) on top of that. v4 is the "Tier-2 accuracy push": log-transformed target (T2-A), dwell-weighted hop ratios (T2-B), extreme-dwell flags/log-transforms (T2-C), corridor reliability scores (T2-D), hub×TOD interactions (T2-E), explicit interaction terms (T2-F), plus the two-stage extreme/normal architecture and the RF+XGB+LGB stacking ensemble. Your printed baseline numbers show RF-T1 (log target) at 28.30 min MAE / 73.9% and RF-T2 at 28.12 min MAE / 73.9% — so T2's marginal gain over T1 in the *simple* log-target RF is actually small; the bigger v4 story is the log-target switch itself and the ensemble/two-stage architecture on top. For the exact v3-vs-v4 delta as officially benchmarked, pull the `results` list from `full_benchmark_summary.csv` — I don't have that exact printed table in what you gave me, so don't quote a v3→v4 number you can't back up live.

## 2. Data understanding & cleaning

**Q7. Why these specific cleaning thresholds?**
A: `segment_actual_time >= 0` and `segment_osrm_time/distance > 0` are basic sanity filters — you can't have negative elapsed time or a zero-distance/zero-time OSRM leg without it being a data artifact. `factor <= 10` caps the trip-level actual/OSRM ratio at 10x — anything beyond that is almost certainly a data logging error rather than real delay signal, and letting it through would let a handful of extreme outliers dominate MAE. `segment_factor >= 0` guards the segment-level equivalent. 10x (rather than 5x or 20x) looks like a judgment call to keep the top of the *real* delay distribution (your worst legitimate corridor tops out around 9.9x per the chronic corridors chart) while cutting clear logging errors — I'd frame it as "chosen to preserve genuine extreme-delay signal while removing implausible outliers," and if pushed, concede it wasn't grid-searched, it was a reasonable domain cutoff.

**Q8. `factor` vs `segment_factor`.**
A: `factor` is the trip-level actual/OSRM time ratio (equivalent to what's later computed explicitly as `delay_ratio`); `segment_factor` is the same ratio at the individual hop level. Filtering `factor` at the trip level catches trips that are anomalous overall; filtering `segment_factor` separately catches a single bad hop that could be an anomaly even if the trip total looks fine, or vice versa — you want both filters since one doesn't imply the other.

**Q9. Mean 2.12x vs median 1.86x, max 77.39x — what does the gap tell you?**
A: A large mean-median gap with a very long right tail is the signature of a heavily right-skewed distribution — a small number of extreme delays are dragging the mean up well above the typical trip. That's exactly why the target was log-transformed (T2-A: predict `log1p(actual_time)`, invert with `expm1`) rather than modeling raw minutes — MSE-style loss on the raw scale would let the model over-focus on minimizing error for the rare 50x-delay trips at the expense of getting the *typical* trip right. It's also why a separate two-stage model was built that explicitly classifies "extreme" trips (delay > 3x) and gives them their own regressor, rather than asking one model to be good at both a 20-minute local hop and a 10-hour stuck-in-transit shipment simultaneously.

**Q10. Is a 94.7% breach rate still a meaningful label?**
A: Fair challenge — if "SLA breach" describes 19 out of 20 trips, it's not discriminating much on its own, and as a *binary label* it's nearly constant. But it's still useful in two ways: as a component of the composite corridor-risk and bottleneck scores (combined with delay *variance*, not used alone), and as a diagnostic that the *threshold itself* — 1.2x OSRM — may be miscalibrated for this network, since it implies OSRM's baseline assumption (clean traffic, zero dwell) essentially never holds in practice. A next step would be to either recalibrate the SLA multiplier against the achievable baseline (e.g., the 25th percentile of actual delay ratios) or move to a continuous risk score instead of a binary breach flag as the primary label — which is in fact what `corridor_risk_score` does.

**Q11. Fallback chain for a hub in test but not train.**
A: Take `hub_dwell_median` as the example. First the code merges `hub_dwell` (train-only aggregates) onto the row — if the hub isn't in that table, the merge produces `NaN`. Then `.fillna(global_dwell_median)` substitutes the *global* median across all train hubs (reported as 12.0 min). Same pattern for `corridor_delay_median`, but there the fallback is two-deep: try the corridor-specific median, then fall back to that hub's overall delay median (`hub_delay_map`), then fall back to the global delay median if even the hub itself is unseen — a "most specific available prior" pattern: corridor > hub > global.

**Q12. Why compute train-only stats and merge onto both, not recompute on the full dataset?**
A: Recomputing on the full dataset (train+test combined) would leak test-set outcomes into features used to predict on the test set — e.g., a corridor's `corridor_delay_median` would partly reflect the *actual* delay of the very test rows being predicted, silently inflating reported test accuracy. Fitting all aggregates strictly on `df_train` and merging (not recomputing) onto `df_test` keeps the test set an honest, unseen hold-out. (This doesn't fully eliminate leakage *within* the training set itself — see Section 10, Q2.)

## 3. Graph construction & network analysis

**Q13. Nodes, edges, and the `dwell_adj_weight` formula.**
A: Nodes are facilities (hubs/centers); a directed edge exists for every observed `source_center → destination_center` corridor, weighted by `dwell_adj_weight = median_delay_ratio * (1 + mean_dwell / max_dwell)`. It's multiplicative rather than additive because dwell needed to act as a *scaling* penalty on top of the existing delay ratio, not an independent additive cost on a different scale (minutes vs. a unitless ratio) — multiplying by `(1 + mean_dwell/max_dwell)` keeps the result unitless and bounds the dwell contribution between 1x (no dwell) and 2x (max dwell in the network) rather than letting a 600-minute outlier hub blow the weight up arbitrarily.

**Q14. Why directed, and would undirected change rankings much?**
A: Directed because a source→destination corridor isn't symmetric in practice — dwell time, delay ratio, and trip volume can differ meaningfully by direction. Betweenness on the undirected version would likely *increase* scores for hubs that are highly connected in only one direction, since undirected shortest paths have more route options to pass through them — the top-line ranking (Gurgaon, Bangalore, Hyderabad) would probably stay similar since they're dominant either way, but the tail rankings would shift.

**Q15. Betweenness computational complexity and performance.**
A: Exact betweenness via Brandes' algorithm is O(V·E) for unweighted graphs, worse for weighted (Dijkstra-based per source). At ~1,261 nodes and ~2,500 edges in the main component, that's tractable to run exactly without sampling — `nx.betweenness_centrality(G_main, normalized=True, weight="weight")` was called without the `k` parameter, i.e., exact computation over all node pairs, not an approximation. If the graph were 10-100x larger, I'd switch to `k`-sampled betweenness (a random subset of source nodes) to keep runtime bounded.

**Q16. Is Gurgaon's outsized 0.359 vs. next-highest 0.219 expected?**
A: Yes — this is exactly the expected shape of a hub-and-spoke logistics network. A small number of super-hubs sit on a disproportionate share of shortest paths by design, since the network is built to consolidate traffic through them. It's not a data artifact; it's the actual topology. It *is* a single-point-of-failure risk operationally, and from a feature-scaling standpoint, betweenness is normalized by its max value in the bottleneck score (`betweenness / betweenness.max()`) specifically so this one dominant hub doesn't distort the composite score's scale for everyone else.

**Q17. Why compute PageRank, degree, and clustering if they correlate with betweenness?**
A: They measure different things. Betweenness = how often shortest paths pass through you (bridge/bottleneck role). PageRank = how much traffic-weighted importance flows to you (destination importance, propagates recursively). Degree = raw connectivity. Clustering coefficient = whether your neighbors talk to each other — low clustering flags structural bridges. Clustering is used explicitly as the "bridge" term (`1 - clustering_coeff`) in the bottleneck score precisely because a hub with low clustering is more likely to be a chokepoint with no alternate local routing.

**Q18. What does a Node2Vec embedding for a facility actually encode?**
A: Node2Vec runs biased random walks over the graph and trains a skip-gram model (like word2vec) so nodes that co-occur on similar walks end up close together in embedding space. Two facilities get similar 16-dim embeddings if they play a similar *structural role* in the network — similar neighborhoods, similar position relative to hubs — even if they're not directly connected. That's richer than betweenness/degree alone because it can capture higher-order neighborhood patterns that a single centrality scalar collapses away.

**Q19. Is the PCA fallback a reasonable substitute for Node2Vec?**
A: Only partially. PCA on `[betweenness, in_degree, out_degree, clustering, pagerank]` is a linear dimensionality reduction of centrality scalars already used elsewhere as separate features — it can't discover the higher-order neighborhood-similarity structure random-walk embeddings capture, since it has no access to *which specific nodes* a facility connects to, only its aggregate connectivity stats. It's a reasonable engineering fallback so the pipeline doesn't break without `node2vec` installed, but it's a degraded substitute, not an equivalent — which lines up with what the feature importance chart shows (see next answer).

**Q20. Node2Vec dims rank near-zero importance — was it worth it?**
A: Fair catch: in the final joint model, the `n2v_*` dimensions contribute almost nothing once distance/OSRM-time and the simpler engineered features are already in the model — likely because their signal is redundant with betweenness/PageRank/dwell, which the model can access more directly. I'd defend keeping it in the writeup for two reasons: it was a legitimate hypothesis worth testing given the network structure, and "we tried structural embeddings and found the simpler centrality features already captured the useful signal" is a real, honest finding, not a failure. I wouldn't claim it as a driver of the accuracy gain on a resume bullet, though — that claim isn't supported by the feature importance chart.

**Q21. How were the bottleneck score weights chosen?**
A: They're domain-judgment weights, not tuned/optimized — `0.30*betweenness + 0.25*sla_breach_rate + 0.20*degree_total + 0.10*(1-clustering) + 0.10*hub_dwell_median + 0.05*corridor_risk`, all min-max normalized. Betweenness gets the largest weight because structural cascading risk is the thing OSRM/simple stats can't see. If pushed to defend it rigorously, the right next step would be a sensitivity analysis — re-rank hubs under a few different weight sets and check if the top-5 is stable — rather than asserting these exact numbers are correct. (Also see Section 10, Q1 — a second script in this project uses a genuinely different 5-term formula, so be ready to reconcile that.)

**Q22. Weakly vs strongly connected components — why WCC here?**
A: A directed graph is strongly connected if every node can reach every other node *and* be reached back; weakly connected if the graph would be connected ignoring edge direction. Logistics networks are closer to a DAG-like hub-and-spoke structure — most nodes won't have a return path through the same route — so requiring strong connectivity would fragment the graph into many tiny components. WCC is the right notion here because it captures "is this facility part of the same overall reachable network," not "can traffic flow symmetrically between any two facilities."

## 4. Feature engineering (T1 / T2)

**Q23. `dwell_to_osrm_ratio` in one sentence.**
A: It's the fraction of a trip's OSRM-estimated travel time consumed just by facility dwell (loading/unloading/sorting) that OSRM never modeled at all — a mean of 3.37 means expected dwell across a trip's hops is, on average, 3.37 times the entire OSRM time estimate, i.e., the "hidden" hub-processing time dwarfs the routing time itself.

**Q24. Why both log-transform and clip the same dwell feature?**
A: They serve different purposes rather than being redundant. `hub_dwell_log` (`log1p`) compresses the full range including the 668-minute outlier hub so a linear/Ridge-style model doesn't get dominated by one extreme value, while preserving some signal from it. `hub_dwell_clipped` (capped at 100 min) instead treats "extremely bad" as a flat ceiling — useful for tree-based splits where you want a clean, bounded feature for the *normal-range* hubs without the extreme tail warping split points. Plus `src_is_extreme_dwell` as an explicit binary flag for cases where the exact magnitude above 100 min doesn't matter, just that it's extreme.

**Q25. Why hand-engineer interactions when trees learn them automatically?**
A: Trees can approximate interactions through sequential splits, but only if there's enough data density to support deep enough splits, and only implicitly — they have to rediscover the interaction via trial-and-error partitioning. Explicitly engineering `dwell_x_distance`, `corridor_x_tod`, `dwell_ratio_x_breach` gives the model a direct, cleanly-scaled signal for well-known coupling effects rather than hoping the tree stumbles onto the right sequence of splits, and it also helps linear/Ridge-style models (like the stacking meta-learner) that can't learn interactions on their own at all.

**Q26. Why clip the distance denominator at 0.1 instead of guarding distance==0 directly?**
A: `(distance/100).clip(0.1)` avoids both a divide-by-zero *and* a divide-by-near-zero blowup for very short trips. Clipping the denominator caps the maximum possible value of the ratio rather than just preventing a hard crash, which is a more robust guard than an `if distance == 0` check that would still let a 1-meter trip produce a nonsensical ratio.

**Q27. Why the 5–11 / 12–20 / 21–4 TOD buckets, and were they validated?**
A: They're domain-driven — morning dispatch/rush, working-day afternoon, overnight/night operations — a standard logistics shift framing, not derived from a data-driven changepoint search. I didn't run a formal validation (checking if delay actually changes at exactly hour 5 or 21 vs. hour 6 or 22); a reasonable operational shift boundary, and a follow-up would be to bin by hour and look for the actual breakpoints in mean delay before finalizing the buckets.

**Q28. `is_night = hour.between(7,20)` — naming looks backwards.**
A: That's correct — that line computes whether the hour falls in the 7am–8pm daytime window, but the variable is named `is_night`, which is a naming bug: the logic and the name are inverted. It doesn't corrupt the feature's information content (the model still sees a valid binary day/night split, just inverted from what the name implies), but it's exactly the kind of thing that causes confusion in code review. Fix is a one-line rename or logic flip.

**Q29. Where's the leakage boundary in T1/T2 features derived from the target?**
A: The boundary that matters is train→test: hub/corridor aggregates are fit *only* on `df_train` and merged (not recomputed) onto `df_test`, so no test-set actual-time information leaks into test features — test metrics stay trustworthy as an estimate of generalization to new trips over already-seen corridors/hubs. The leakage that *does* exist is internal to the training set: a hub's `hub_dwell_median` is computed by aggregating `segment_delay` across all training rows through that hub, **including the very row the feature is being computed for** — so each training row's feature is partly derived from its own label. This doesn't bias test metrics, but it does mean training-set feature importances could be subtly optimistic. The clean fix is k-fold or leave-one-out target encoding for the training set specifically (Section 9, Q6 has the code).

**Q30. Why 60/40 weights for `corridor_risk_score`?**
A: `corridor_risk_score = 0.6*sla_breach_rate + 0.4*(delay_std/max_delay_std)`. Breach rate gets the larger weight as the more directly business-relevant signal, while delay variance is a secondary "how unpredictable is this corridor" signal — a corridor could have a moderate breach rate but wildly inconsistent delivery times, which is its own operational headache. I didn't run a formal ablation comparing this composite against breach rate alone as a standalone feature — the current weights are a reasonable prior, not an empirically optimized choice.

**Q31. 24 → 69 columns — multicollinearity handling?**
A: No formal VIF checks or feature-dropping before feeding the full 69-feature set into the tree ensembles — tree-based models are fairly robust to multicollinearity, since correlated features just get their importance "split" across redundant copies rather than causing instability. Where it matters more is the Ridge meta-learner — but that's trained on just 2-3 out-of-fold base-learner predictions, not the raw 69 features, and Ridge's L2 penalty handles that correlation gracefully. For a linear model trained directly on the 69 features, I'd want to check correlation/VIF first — log-distance and raw-distance, for instance, are functions of each other and would need addressing.

## 5. Modeling — two-stage architecture & stacking ensemble

**Q32. Why two-stage instead of just adding `is_extreme_trip` as a feature?**
A: The hypothesis was that extreme trips (delay > 3x) are driven by qualitatively different dynamics — dwell/hub-congestion-dominated — versus normal trips, which are more congestion/distance-driven in a "normal" range. Giving each regime its own regressor with its own feature emphasis (Stage-2b explicitly weights dwell features more heavily) lets each model specialize, rather than asking one regressor to learn both regimes simultaneously, where rare extreme cases could be drowned out or, if up-weighted, distort the fit for normal trips.

**Q33. Two-stage (32.65 min MAE) underperforms plain RF-T2 (28.12 min MAE) — why keep/report it?**
A: This is a real, honest result to lead with rather than hide: the two-stage architecture, as built, didn't beat the simpler single log-target RF. My read on why: the soft blend introduces two additional sources of error — the Stage-1 classifier's probability estimate is itself imperfect, and errors in `p_extreme` compound through the blend rather than canceling. There's also less training data for the extreme-trip regressor, so `rf_extreme` is likely noisier than a single RF trained on the full dataset. I'd present this as "we hypothesized a specialized architecture would help, tested it rigorously, and found the simpler unified model actually generalized better" — a disciplined finding, not a failed attempt to hide.

**Q34. Failure modes of the soft-gating blend formula.**
A: Two main ones: if `p_extreme` is miscalibrated, the blend inherits that bias directly since it's a linear weighting, not a hard decision; and for borderline trips where `p_extreme` is near 0.5, the blended prediction is an average of two regressors trained on very different regimes, which can land in a "no-man's-land" resembling neither a typical normal trip nor a typical extreme one. A hard-threshold approach avoids the middle-ground blending problem but introduces a hard discontinuity at the threshold and is sensitive to threshold choice.

**Q35. Is 92.2% Stage-1 accuracy actually good given ~7.8% extreme rate?**
A: No — a trivial classifier that always predicts "not extreme" would score roughly (1 - 0.078) ≈ 92.2% too, suspiciously close to the reported number. Accuracy is the wrong headline metric for this imbalanced binary target; precision/recall/F1 for the *extreme* class specifically would tell the real story. I'd flag this proactively: "the accuracy number is somewhat misleading given the class imbalance — the classification report I printed at runtime has the real precision/recall breakdown, and that's the number I'd actually want to defend."

**Q36. Why different feature sets for `rf_normal` vs `rf_extreme`?**
A: `rf_normal` uses the full T2+graph feature set (distance-heavy) because normal-range delay is closer to congestion/routing-driven. `rf_extreme` uses a dwell-heavy subset (`hub_dwell_log`, `expected_total_dwell`, `dwell_x_distance`, `dwell_x_hops`, `hub_tod_dwell`, etc.) because the hypothesis is that extreme delays are overwhelmingly caused by facility dwell/congestion events, not routing distance. Giving the extreme-trip model a feature set tilted toward the hypothesized causal driver lets it specialize rather than dilute capacity on features that matter more for the normal regime.

**Q37. Why Ridge as the stacking meta-learner?**
A: A natural first choice because the inputs (2-3 base-learner predictions) are typically highly correlated with each other, and Ridge's L2 penalty handles that multicollinearity gracefully by shrinking coefficients rather than letting them destabilize (which plain OLS can do with correlated inputs). It's also simple, fast, and interpretable — `meta.coef_` directly tells you how much the meta-learner trusts each base learner — versus another tree ensemble as meta-learner, which adds complexity without a clear benefit at only 2-3 input features.

**Q38. Why `cross_val_predict` for OOF instead of training on full data and predicting on test directly?**
A: If each base learner is trained on the full training set and then predicts on that same training set to train the meta-learner, those predictions are in-sample and overfit — the meta-learner would learn to over-trust base learners that memorized training noise, and that overconfidence wouldn't show up until test time. 5-fold `cross_val_predict` generates genuinely out-of-fold predictions — each training row's prediction comes from a model that never saw that row during training — so the meta-learner is trained on honestly out-of-sample base predictions, letting it learn a generalizable weighting rather than memorizing quirks.

**Q39. Why fit Ridge on log-scale OOF predictions, not raw-minute predictions?**
A: All three base learners were trained to predict `log1p(actual_time)` directly, so their raw outputs are already in log-space — fitting Ridge on log-scale keeps the meta-learner consistent with the scale the base learners actually optimized against, and avoids introducing a nonlinear distortion (via `expm1`) *before* the linear blend, which would make the relationship between base predictions and target no longer simply linear-additive. Invert with a single `expm1` after the meta-learner's linear combination, at the very end.

**Q40. What would it mean if XGBoost got much higher meta-learner weight than RF, and would you drop RF?**
A: It would mean the meta-learner found XGBoost's OOF predictions more informative/less noisy relative to the target — plausible since gradient boosting often captures fine-grained residual patterns RF's bagged/averaged trees smooth over. I'd be cautious about dropping RF purely based on meta-learner weight, though, since even a lower-weighted base learner can still contribute diversity that improves overall robustness. I'd test that directly — refit the meta-learner without RF and compare test MAE — before deciding to simplify.

**Q41. Best-MAE stack vs. the single RF you feature-importance'd — which would you actually ship?**
A: I'd lean toward the single interpretable RF for production unless the stacking ensemble's MAE improvement is large and consistent enough to justify the added latency, complexity, and maintenance burden of running three models plus a meta-learner in a live prediction path. For an ETA shown to a customer or dispatcher, interpretability and operational simplicity often matter as much as squeezing out an extra fraction of a minute in MAE — I'd want the actual MAE gap and its stability over time before recommending the heavier stack for production.

**Q42. `log_osrm_distance`/`osrm_distance` dominate final importance — doesn't that undercut the 69 engineered features?**
A: A real tension worth explaining clearly rather than getting caught off guard by: the incremental ablation and the final joint feature importance answer different questions. Ablation tells you "how much does this group help when it's the only new signal added" — T1/T2 features clearly helped there (graph alone: +7.1pp; T1: further gains). Final joint importance tells you "how much does the *fitted model actually lean on* this feature relative to everything else present" — once distance/OSRM-time are in the model, many T1/T2 features (correlated with distance, e.g., `dwell_x_distance`, `log_actual_distance`) get "crowded out" in relative importance even though they contributed real signal during ablation, especially when tree splits on raw distance already implicitly capture some of what those engineered features encode. Distance is unavoidably the strongest raw predictor; the engineered features' real value shows up as *modulating* distance's effect rather than competing with it for top-line importance.

## 6. Model evaluation & the "74% accuracy" claim

**Q43. Why "% within 15% of actual" over RMSE/MAE as the primary KPI?**
A: MAE and RMSE are useful diagnostics, but neither directly answers the business question: "is this prediction close enough to be useful?" A relative-error threshold metric translates model performance into an operationally meaningful yes/no per trip, easier to communicate to non-technical stakeholders than "27.9 minutes MAE" in isolation — a 27.9-minute error means something very different on a 30-minute local delivery versus a 20-hour interstate haul, and a relative threshold naturally accounts for that scale difference.

**Q44. Is 27.9 min MAE good or bad, contextualized?**
A: Given trips range from short local hops to multi-hour interstate hauls with a median delay ratio around 1.86x, a ~28-minute average error is a meaningful, usable improvement, especially paired with 74.2% of predictions landing within 15% of actual — meaning for roughly three out of four trips the ETA is genuinely close, and the 28-minute figure is being pulled up by harder, longer-tail trips. I'd want the actual mean/median `actual_time` pulled up live (`df_train['actual_time'].mean()`) to give a precise "% of average trip length" framing rather than guess a number I don't have handy.

**Q45. Asymmetric error: overestimate >50% (3.1%) vs underestimate >50% (1.5%).**
A: The model is more prone to substantially *overestimating* delay than underestimating it by a large margin. Plausible given the heavy right tail (max delay ratio 77x): a log-target model that's learned "some trips are extremely delayed" can occasionally over-apply that lesson to a trip that's actually going to be normal, producing an overestimate. Underestimating by >50% is rarer because strong signal (dwell features, Stage-1's extreme classifier) points the model toward *expecting* delay, so it rarely misses in the optimistic direction by a wide margin. For SLA-setting, the current asymmetry is actually the safer direction — overestimating delay is better for setting customer expectations than underestimating and then breaching a promised ETA.

**Q46. Explain the ablation methodology and ordering-dependence concern.**
A: The ablation adds feature groups progressively — base OSRM → + graph centrality → + T1 → + T2 — measuring the MAE/accuracy delta at each step and attributing it to the newly-added group. This is standard for telling an incremental-improvement story, but it's genuinely ordering-dependent: because features are correlated (graph betweenness correlates with hub dwell), the contribution "attributed" to graph would look different if T1 had been added first and graph second. ## 12. Rapid-fire ML/stats concepts

**Log-transform vs. robust loss for skewed targets:** Log-transforming reshapes the target distribution itself to be more symmetric/homoscedastic, helping every downstream model uniformly (including linear models in the stack). A robust loss (Huber, quantile) instead changes how the model penalizes residuals but leaves the raw skewed target and its heteroscedastic variance intact — log-transform is preferred when skew reflects genuine multiplicative/percentage-scale effects (as delay ratios do here), while robust losses are better when outliers should specifically be down-weighted as noise rather than reshaping genuine signal.

**Bias-variance via `min_samples_leaf`:** Small `min_samples_leaf` (e.g., 1) lets trees fit very specific, small groups of points — low bias, high variance (overfits noise). `min_samples_leaf=3` forces each leaf to average over at least 3 points, trading a small amount of bias for meaningfully reduced variance — a reasonable middle ground given ~100K+ training rows, plenty of data to support slightly larger leaves without starving any region of feature space.

**Why Ridge over Lasso/plain linear for the meta-learner:** Lasso's L1 penalty would zero out one of only 2-3 highly-correlated base-learner predictions somewhat arbitrarily, throwing away useful ensemble diversity; Ridge's L2 penalty shrinks and shares weight across correlated predictors — the more appropriate behavior when the blend should use all base learners' signal, not select a sparse subset. Plain OLS with no penalty is unstable when inputs are this correlated.

**`expm1`/`log1p` numerical stability:** `log1p(x) = log(1+x)` and `expm1(x) = exp(x)-1` avoid catastrophic cancellation for small `x` near zero — naive `log(1+x)` loses precision when `x` is tiny because `1+x` rounds to `1.0` in floating point before the log is taken, while `log1p` handles that regime accurately. Given many delay/dwell values here are legitimately close to zero, this protects real precision, not just style.

**MAE vs RMSE vs "% within 15%":** MAE weights all errors linearly (robust to outliers, easy to interpret in minutes). RMSE squares errors, penalizing large misses much more heavily (sensitive to the 77x-outlier trips) — useful if large errors are disproportionately costly. "% within 15%" translates error into a business-relevant hit/miss threshold, ignoring magnitude beyond the threshold — best as the headline stakeholder metric, alongside MAE/RMSE as technical diagnostics, not instead of them.

**Betweenness vs PageRank vs degree in one sentence each:** Betweenness = how often you sit on the shortest path between other node pairs (bridge/chokepoint role). PageRank = how much importance recursively flows to you from other important nodes (destination prestige). Degree = how many direct connections you have (raw local connectivity). A node can rank #1 on degree but low on betweenness if its neighbors also connect directly to each other (high local clustering, so paths don't need to route through it) — why clustering coefficient and betweenness are inversely related in the bottleneck score's "bridge" term.

**Node2Vec vs DeepWalk, and why p/q matter for a directed graph:** DeepWalk generates random walks with uniform transition probability to any neighbor; Node2Vec adds two bias parameters — `p` (return, controls revisiting the previous node) and `q` (in-out, controls BFS-like local exploration vs DFS-like outward exploration) — letting you tune whether embeddings emphasize local community structure or broader structural-role similarity. For a directed logistics graph, this matters because embeddings should reflect a facility's role (feeder vs. consolidation hub) rather than just immediate neighborhood, favoring DFS-like exploration (low `q`) over pure local BFS.

**Why GradientBoostingClassifier for Stage-1 but RandomForestRegressor for Stage-2 — intentional or convenience?** Honest answer over an invented principled justification: this looks like a reasonable-but-not-rigorously-justified choice — gradient boosting classifiers often edge out RF on binary classification with careful tuning, a defensible reason to pick it for Stage-1, but no controlled comparison (RF classifier vs GB classifier, both tuned, same Stage-1 task) was run to prove GB was actually better here versus just being what was reached for. "It was a reasonable default choice for classification, not an empirically validated one — a natural next step would be to A/B the classifier choice directly."
